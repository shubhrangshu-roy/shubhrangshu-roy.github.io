THE ECONOMIC TIMES / In Good Company

I’m luvn’ it!


2000-2006

Shubhrangshu Roy

I’m luvin’ it! And how? To stretch a short story long, I’ve just discovered the transmigration of the soul inside the Big Mac burger. Lemme guide you there. Only the other day, Vikram Bakshi, McDonald’s franchisee in town, walked into our office, PowerPoint, projector, PR et al, proclaiming that in the 10 years that it’s been around, McDonald’s worked wonders in India when KFC failed. To me, it looks the other way around. India’s worked wonders for McDonald’s when it failed just about every other multinational fast-food joint. And there’s a lesson in it for the mascot Ronald McDonald. I’ll tell him how. But, first things, first.

There’s a certainty to Mr Bakshi’s McBoast. And given the empirical evidence he furnishes there’s little doubt he has been a win-win story so far. In the decade it’s been around, McDonald’s has grown from two outlets in Delhi to 85 restaurants and four food courts across north and west India, each posting an average revenue of Rs 2.5 crore a year; its gross turnover growing at 40% CAGR.

What’s more, unlike other urban American legends that have been pasted all over, pelted with stones or battered by commies and communalists alike, I don’t quite know of anyone around who’s even raised an eyebrow at Americana’s biggest symbol in India. Small wonder then, Mr Bakshi says, India’s emerging as the fastest growth story among the 31,000 McDonald’s outlets across the world. And this is only the start. The future ought to be bigger. What’s up?

If I bought the McDonald’s story, Indians are among the world’s youngest people with huge consumption expenses and discretionary spending habits. Of every 100 Indians who go out shopping, 70 eat out as well, and 40 seek outdoor entertainment. They are now splurging on McDonald’s. So far, so good. But that’s old hat too. If Indians are biting into McDonald’s, there’s no reason why they shouldn’t be digging into KFC as well. And that’s where the real story lies hidden, literally between the buns. KFC bombed India because it tried replacing our desi tandoori kukkad with American fried chicken. McDonald’s worked because it replaced the burger inside its buns.

Two years into its India launch, McDonald’s first replaced its traditional beef patties with mutton burgers. But when that failed, it went for a soul transplant, keeping its image intact. McDonald’s scripted its dramatic India story out of our grandma’s home recipe; at 16% of total sales, its McAloo Tikki Burger is a chartbuster and the single-biggest- selling products in India, followed, of course, by Veg Pizza McPuff. What does it say?

McDonald’s worked in India because despite its all-American golden arches, it sublimated its existence to the quintessential Indian consciousness, delinking its body from its soul. And it did so 50 years after it first set shop across America selling hamburgers that trace their origins 800 years earlier to Mongol warlord Genghis Khan’s camp, before sublimating Russian tastebuds, and then, sailing into New York harbour two centuries ago via Hamburg, and finally reinvading Russia and China, besides 117 other countries, is the $20-billion great American 20th-century fast food wonder served 50 million customers each day.

Yet, in the 50 years that it’s successfully been around, there’s been more to McDonald’s than burgers: Children in the US, Spain and England has been reported to have suffered food poisoning, eating E.coli-infected beef burgers. McDonald’s has been accused of paying sub-minimum wages. And it’s been the subject of the McLibel, the longest trial in English legal history. The verdict delivered in June 1997, ruled that McDonald’s “exploit children’ with their advertising, produce ‘misleading’ advertising, are ‘culpably responsible’ for cruelty to animals, are ‘antipathetic’ to unionisation and pay their worker's low wages. A further court ruling to an appeal in ’99 pronounced: “If one eats enough McDonald’s food, one’s diet may well become high in fat etc., with the very real risk of heart disease.” In just about the time that it’s been in India, McLibel went on the spawn anti-McDonald’s campaign worldwide. A feature-length documentary was broadcast across the world, and the McLibel website was born. Stung by these charges, McDonald’s announced early this year upcoming menu changes that will emphasise chicken, salads, and other fresh food rather than hamburgers. How I wish, they opt for McAloo, instead. Now that it’s found its soul in India, the good ol’ burger can seek global salvation at last. For you can find the seeds of global consciousness in, of all things, the aloo. A native of Peru, the potato transmigrated to Europe abroad Spanish colonial vessels to become a Continental staple. The Irish transplanted it to North America, while the Portuguese brought it to India.

To quote the scriptures: aham brahmasmi, tatvam asi. I am the consciousness, and so are you. For McDonald’s, that consciousness has got to be the aloo!
