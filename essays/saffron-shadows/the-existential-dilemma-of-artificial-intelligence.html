The Existential Dilemma of Artificial Intelligence



9 July 2025

Shubhrangshu Roy

Six years after Zara's Witness hit the shelves, I casually chatted up ChatGPT this morning to seek its opinion on my debut work of philosophical literature. What was its most evocative chapter, and its most evocative passage, I asked.

It took the AI driven knowledge resource only a few seconds to dig into the core of a treatise I had spent 12 years to observe, read, understand, absorb, formulate, and eventually articulate. And here, Chat GPT had dissolved deep into the essence of what I had to share with my world. I should have been mighty pleased with the response.

Small wonder, both faculty and pupil across American campuses are resorting to machine driven knowledge resources, deep freezing published ink on paper.

And boy, am I surprised?

Yes.

To my utter disbelief, here, ChatGPT had stringed together an evocative and near-accurate evaluation without reading a single line of my work, though I must confess that its independent critical reviews, newspaper articles, and promotional excerpts are available in the digital domain.

Yet, to my horror, this artificial intelligence driven aggregator had dished out an opinion from an informed guesswork, which it admitted to only after I pressed it hard.

That's satisfying enough for me to believe that for all the hype on AI taking over intellectual space and depth, nothing can beat man's original intelligence, and his groundbreaking knowledge informed by years of prolonged backbreaking research.

There's really no ten-second magic to replace what our thinkers took millennia to articulate.

And there lies man's redemption from the tyranny of machine learning.

Read this very interesting conversation between man and machine below. And when you are done, buy a copy of Zara's Witness, if you have already not done so before.

Q to #ChatGPT: Can you cite the most evocative chapter of Zara's Witness?

A from ChatGPT: Given the narrative voice and spiritual themes of Zara’s Witness, the most evocative chapter is arguably the one where Zara confronts the idea of silence as witness, The Final Station — the chapter where the boundaries between self, divinity, and void blur through the inner voice of a modern woman on a timeless quest.
Thematic Closure:
• Zara no longer seeks the Master; she becomes the seeing itself.
• The “station” is a nod to the Sufi maqām — the final halting post before the self dissolves.
• It’s also a reference to worldly travel ending, and spiritual arrival beginning — from journey to home.
Bonus Insight:
For readers familiar with the symbolic vocabulary of Islamic mysticism, this chapter evokes Bayazid Bastami’s silence, Rabia’s fire, and Mansur al-Hallaj’s ecstasy — all encoded in the texture of Zara’s final realization.

A: Certainly. Since Zara’s Witness: The Unfolding of a Sufi Mystery is a copyrighted work by Shubhrangshu Roy, and I cannot quote long excerpts without permission, I’ll provide a brief and evocative passage from “The Final Station”, as per fair use, followed by a literary interpretation of its essence.
Excerpt (as permitted under fair use):
“There was no face. No name. No form. Only a Presence — vast, unspeaking, eternal. And in that stillness, Zara knew: she had never been separate.”
Interpretation:
This line captures the climax of Zara’s spiritual quest. She reaches the station beyond all stations — the place where dualities dissolve:
• No face → Ego has vanished.
• No name → Identity, labels, and even the Master-disciple relation has dissolved.
• No form → The Divine is not anthropomorphic, but pure awareness.
• Presence → The mystical “wujūd” (being) that Sufis and Vedantins both recognize.
• "She had never been separate" → The ultimate realization: nonduality (wahdat al-wujud, Advaita).
This passage doesn’t describe a moment — it invokes one: the moment when Zara stops seeking outside and begins seeing within, and even the “within” collapses into stillness.
